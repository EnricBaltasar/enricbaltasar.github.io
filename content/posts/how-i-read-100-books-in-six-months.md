<h2 id="isn-t-it-better-to-read-less-quantity-vs-quality"><strong>Isn't it better to read less? Quantity vs quality</strong></h2>
<p>Short answer: no.</p>
<p>Long answer: it depends.</p>
<p>A few books into my challenge, I did some googling on how to read faster to optimise my time. The result?</p>
<p>Disappointment.</p>
<p><strong>I mostly found hacks that made me think of reading like it was fast food. However, if you are what you eat... do you want to be a ‚Ç¨1 McDonald's burger?</strong></p>
<p>I hope you don't. Better to be a real Turkish kebab, for example.</p>
<p>The issue is... quantity matters. When they say size doesn't matter, that' a lie. What they really mean is it's not everything, but a very important factor. Let me tell you story:</p>
<p>At the University of Florida, professor Jerry Uelsmann divided his photography class into two groups for an experimental assignment. Everyone on the left side of the classroom was in the ‚Äúquantity‚Äù group and they had to shoot very frequently, taking as much as 100 photos per day. On the right side, there was the "quality" group, and their task was to capture only the best photos they could, with just 1 a day.</p>
<p><strong>What group do you think submitted the best photos, the ones focused on volume or on quality?</strong></p>
<p>*drum roll*</p>
<p>The quantity group got the best quality pictures.</p>
<p>I read this experiment in book Atomic Habits by James Clear (you can see a <a href="https://jamesclear.com/repetitions" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">story extract here</a>). And I must agree with the findings, because I get to the same conclusion in my life:</p>
<p>üü¢ To get fluent in a language, practice it as much as possible.</p>
<p>üü¢ There is a high correlation between a salesperson's activity (e.g. calls) and their results.</p>
<p><strong>Volume is not everything, but it's key.</strong></p>
<p>You should set the bar high, but not too high you need to be already a giant to get there.</p>
<p>My recommendation for you is to forget the misleading hacks. Strategy is more important.</p>
<p>Growth design is a new way of planning. One focused on practice, learning and iteration. One that makes you go forward faster, more confidently and more aware of yourself. It's the real way to reduce failure and also the master-to-slave ratio on human biology. For example, to improve your long-term memory.</p>
<p><strong>Read about what you're passionate about</strong>, so that you'll want to read. <strong>Focus on a topic you want to implement</strong>, so that you'll remember more.</p>
<p>Don't be a ‚Ç¨1 McDonald's burger. At the same time, always go for more.</p>
<p>By the way, are you aware of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pareto_distribution" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Pareto principle</a>? It states that, for many outcomes, roughly 80% of consequences come from 20% of causes. For example, that about 80% of the revenue in a store are generated by nearly 20% of products. You can apply this to almost anything.</p>
<p>For example, I rated the books I read from 1 to 5. And the result is clear: <strong>if I only read 25% of the books, I'd have obtained 75% of the benefits</strong>. The only issue is... it's difficult to know what'll bring you the best benefit.</p>
<p><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-10186" src="/wp-content/uploads/2030/10/Book-rating.png" alt="" width="600" height="371" /></p>
<p>Let's address the painful question straightforwardly: <strong>how many books were worth reading?</strong> From the statistics, less than half (48%). However, I truly believe the rest of the readings also contributed to my growth, because I was exposed to more ideas, writing styles and point of views.</p>
<p>The ability to compare is part of what makes you an expert.</p>

<h2 id="the-7-steps-to-accelerated-reading"><strong>The 7 steps to accelerated reading</strong></h2>
<p><em>Read 200 pages per hour</em>: this is one of many sales-y promises you'll find out there. If someone reads so fast, chances are that person is reading the wrong books. Too easy to be valuable for your life. You should focus on your personal growth, not on impressing other people.</p>
<h3 id="easy-hacks"><strong>Easy hacks</strong></h3>
<p>Hacks on speed reading are like fast food: easy to buy and nice for a while, but not good for anything. They include activities like avoiding re-reading sentences to reflect, so that you go forward faster (danger! you're limiting your thinking and implementation).</p>
<p>However, here I share with you some tricks that might be helpful in specific situations:</p>
<p><strong>üî¥ Go for many books but only read some chapters of each one</strong>. You'll make a better impression e.g. for a homework because you look like you've researched a lot more, although you actually understand a lot fewer. It's mere superficiality.</p>
<p><strong>üî¥ Skim or scan the text</strong>: look for keywords that you think are more important. You save time by ignoring what you believe is less important.</p>
<h3 id="tactics-you-need"><strong>Tactics you need</strong></h3>
<p>Now yes, here you have some useful tactics you need to implement. This is your healthy and tasty food:</p>
<p><strong>üü¢ Stop slowly speaking when reading</strong> (subvocalising). Gain awareness on the fact you silently pronounce what you read and break the habit. For instance, you can use a pencil or your finger as a guide and move quickly through the text.</p>
<p><strong>üü¢ Don't read the introduction chapter</strong>. It doesn't teach you anything most of the times.</p>
<p><strong>üü¢ Control your unconscious associations</strong>. For example, if you work from home, read from your desk instead of your bed, so that you won't feel lazy o tired.</p>
<p><strong>üü¢ Actively work on your passive reading</strong>. Let me explain: you need to do something else than reading to remember and reflect better. Some people recommend to write a summary after each chapter, but I think it's not necessary unless you're one of those guys who talk about books to make a living. I think it's enough to highlight, make notes and also pauses to analyse how you can implement what you're reading.</p>
<p><b>üü¢ Avoid unnecessary distractions</b> with environmental planning. For example, silence your phone and leave it some meters away. Also, read in a Kindle, so that you won't switch task to social chatting.</p>
<h3 id="the-3-disciplines-of-effective-reading"><strong>The 3 disciplines of effective reading</strong></h3>
<p><img class="aligncenter wp-image-10230" src="/wp-content/uploads/2030/10/intervention-bias-e1633874265935.png" alt="" width="700" height="305" /></p>
<p>There are 3 cornerstones of effective reading:</p>
<p>üí™ To read.</p>
<p>üí™ To understand.</p>
<p>üí™ To retain.</p>
<p>Whatever you do, keep that in mind. For example, if you read a lot but forget what you read after a few months, I'm not sure you're getting much benefit from your effort.</p>
<p><strong>The more you read, the faster you read. The more you learn about a topic, the more you understand. And the more you can implement short-term what you read, the more you'll remember.</strong></p>
<p>There are also other variables, like language mastery. I had already read a significant amount of books in English (my 5th language) by the time the challenge started. However, after reading 97 books in English, I'm pretty sure I read in it faster now.</p>
<p><em>Remark: although a different topic, a small piece of advice if you're interested in reading faster in a foreign language is to focus on understanding the text as a whole and not trying to understand every word.</em></p>

<h2 id="how-to-read-faster-strategic-reading"><strong>How to read faster: strategic reading</strong></h2>
<p>I wanted to learn a lot, mainly what I don't know that I don't know, so I thought about setting a target of 100 books. And I wanted to make it happen, so I decided about a one year timeframe.</p>
<p>The issue was that to consume 100 books in one year was quite a big challenge, so I needed to break it into smaller pieces to make it more manageable, and therefore achievable.</p>
<p>You'll find my new approaches below:</p>
<figure><img src="/wp-content/uploads/2030/10/perspective-shift.jpeg" alt=""><figcaption>Source: <a href="https://www.hscollective.org/news/timeline/the-shadow-of-ignorance/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">hscollective.org</a></figcaption></figure>
<h3 id="i-wouldn-t-read-100-books"><strong>I wouldn't read 100 books</strong></h3>
<p>Human's primitive brain works more easily short-term, but one year was too long and 100 books meant nothing for my daily life.</p>
<p>I asked myself how much I should read every month, every week, every day, comparing books and pages. After some calibration, I came up with the following idea:</p>
<p>üìö How many weeks a year: 365/7 = 52.14 ‚Üí 52 (marginally reduce the time).</p>
<p>üìö How many books a week: 100/52 = 1.92 ‚Üí 2 (marginally increase the objective).</p>
<p><strong>So I set the goal of reading 100 in a year but I'd run for the objective of 2 per week. It made the challenge more tangible and meaningful.</strong></p>
<p>This is the same technique I use with the salespeople I manage at work to make them more accountable and motivated: translate quarterly or even monthly goals to weekly and daily objectives. For example, to make at least 40 calls a day and arrange at least 5 appointments a week.</p>
<h3 id="it-wouldn-t-take-1-year"><strong>It wouldn't take 1 year</strong></h3>
<p>I also give real-time access to statistics that show my salespeople their activity volume and accomplishments. This is for a reason: you can't improve what you don't measure, and you need a system to measure well.</p>
<p>In other words: you need take control over your challenge, and I didn't have that.</p>
<p>After some time, I started to doubt how many I had already read. So I decided to start self-tracking my progress in a spreadsheet given a baseline of 2 books a week, and then compare with the real number in a graph.</p>
<p><strong>It felt good to visually see I was consistently winning over my ghost competitor: the bottom line of 2 books a week.</strong></p>
<p><img class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-10209" src="/wp-content/uploads/2030/10/track-progress-reading.png" alt="" width="1024" height="632" /></p>
<p><strong>I also set a formula to sum up the total number of read books</strong>. This is because there was a lot of time left, and some good vibes were necessary to keep going! Each time that I felt like "uf, still a lot left", I looked at the "Already read" figure.</p>
<p>But there's more: when I hire new salespeople, I tell them I don't expect them to reach the normal level of results in their first week or even month. That takes time. They should do the best they can but not stress. The only important matter for me is to see some sort of progress, even if not reflected on those specific success metrics.</p>
<figure><img src="/wp-content/uploads/2030/10/sales-metrics.png" alt=""><figcaption>Source: <a href="https://www.pipedrive.com/en/blog/sales-metrics" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Pipedrive</a></figcaption></figure>

<h3 id="i-d-follow-my-own-rhythm"><b>I'd follow my own¬†rhythm</b></h3>
<p>We have a beautiful saying in Catalan: <em>tota pedra fa paret</em> (literally <em>Each stone makes the wall</em>), which basically means every small bit counts.</p>
<p><img class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-10235" src="/wp-content/uploads/2030/10/small-stones-wall.jpeg" alt="" width="626" height="417" /></p>
<p>If you've ever gone to the gym, you might know how easy it's to fail. You wake up and come up with any silly excuse to not going one day, then another day, then another one. And when you realise, you're eating an ice cream box and failed for another week.</p>
<p>Although this is a situation I've never experienced with my gym routine, of course there are days I feel like I don't want to go at all. But I force myself to go even if for 15 minutes. And just the fact I start makes me do more than I expected when I had opened my eyes.</p>
<p><em>You're very strict with yourself</em>, said my dietician the last time I visited her. However, the results speak for themselves. And I believe in results.</p>
<p>What I want to say is... <strong>try to read a bit every day</strong>.</p>
<p>I didn't track pages or minutes, simply found small chunks of time to read multiple times a day.</p>
<p>You shouldn't micro-manage yourself. I find objectives like reading 20 pages per day or 1 book every three days quite dumb. Just focus on 2 books per week. And if you find a very interesting one that makes you think, no worries if you spend 2 weeks on it.</p>
<p>Remember why you started the challenge in the first place, and focus on the long term. Apply this to any area of your life that matters to you.</p>

<h2 id="what-i-discovered-after-reading-100-books"><strong>What I discovered after reading 100 books</strong></h2>
<p>Here you have some of my conclusions:</p>
<p>üöÄ <strong>Reading 100 books was like learning to sum 2+2 as a kid</strong>. Now, I truly believe I could read a minimum of 500 books a year if I had no job.</p>
<p>üöÄ <strong>New is not better</strong>. <em>Breakthrough Advertising</em> was published in 1966, and one might think advertising has evolved a lot, so that reading a book written over 50 years ago wouldn't be a productive task. But many of my favourite books turned out to be written long ago.¬†</p>
<p>Some of those is what I consider <strong>foundational books</strong>, because a lot of what we do or think today is based on those texts. Close to the concept of a bible in societal terms.</p>
<p>I even read books such as <em>On the origin of species</em> by Darwin, and oh man... I almost stopped to believe in human evolution. That book was crap, full of "I think" statements and lacked a lot of directly provable evidence. It contained a lot of "I have seen this, I have tried this" statements. It almost felt like a Marco Polo trip, not science.</p>
<p>üöÄ <strong>Nearly every successful book is a lie</strong>, sometimes small, other times a big one. People invent frameworks that simplify reality and make it feel easier, simpler. They sell very well, but many schemes are brought to ridiculous extremes. It's just people aren't aware of it.</p>
<p>üöÄ <strong>Sales was an interesting playground</strong> to test my ability to go from nearly zero experience to building a successful system, hire the right people and get tangible results in record time.</p>
<p>üöÄ I learned what makes a book really great: the way of writing, the structure, what keeps readers engaged, the importance of stories and how to create them, etc. Also, how some people build their business around books and how I could do the same profitably. The challenge <b>made me a better reader and writer, and that's awesome because writing skills are undoubtedly important</b> for a marketer like me.</p>
